Mobile CSP Objectives

Description of Lesson Activity

Learning Objective 2.1.1:
Describe the variety of abstractions used to represent data.
BI 2EU 2.1EK 2.1.1bP 3

Students watch a video and a slide presentation to learn that digital images, like all digital data, are composed of bits.

They complete interactive exercise to practice using the run-length encoding technique to express images as numbers and numbers as images.

    Learning Activities

    Hook/Motivation

    Lesson Starter (5 minutes)

    Ask students to think about how images are represented on computers. If no one mentions it, explain computers break down images into pixels, and that the term pixel is short for “picture element.” Discuss image representation with the class, being sure to cover the following points:
    • After breaking an image into pixels, computers use a variety of methods to represent each pixel or groups of pixels as numbers.
    • Numbers are translated to bits, which are stored and sent like any other data.
    • Other computers use the bits to reconstruct image.
    • Storing specific information about every single bit would be an enormous amount of data for an image. What are some ways a computer might store an image using less data?a

    Experiences and Exploration

    CS Unplugged Video (30 minutes)

    Have students watch this CS Unplugged video on image compression for a quick look at one way that images are represented and compressed.

    PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT

    Simple black-and-white images such as fax images, as well as color pictures, have a lot of repetition in them. To reduce amount of the data needed to store and transmit images, computer scientists use a variety of image compression techniques. The technique demonstrated in the video and used in this lesson is known as run-length encoding, or RLE. In this technique, the picture is converted into numbers by recording the length of alternating white and black pixels. This technique is well suited to images that have a lot of repetition in them. For example, fax images often have large blocks of white (e.g. margins) or black pixels (e.g. a horizontal line). Color pictures also have a lot of repetition in them. In practice, programmers choose a compression technique to best suit the image he or she is transmitting.

    From CS Unplugged: “If we didn't compress images it would take much longer to transmit pictures and require much more storage space. This would make it unfeasible to send faxes or put photos on a web page. For example, fax images are generally compressed to about a seventh of their original size, so without compression they would take seven times as long to transmit! Photographs and pictures are often compressed to a tenth or even a hundredth of their original size (using a different technique). This allows many more images to be stored on a disk, and it means that viewing them over the web will take a fraction of the time.”

    Ask students to explain to a partner how RLE works, and then have them check their understanding by completing the interactive exercises on the student site.

    DIFFERENTIATION: More Practice. To provide more practice with RLE, have students complete these CS Unplugged Worksheets (pg. 17-18).

    DIFFERENTIATION: Enrichment. To extend students' understanding of RLE have them complete p. 19 of the CS Unplugged Worksheets (pg. 17-18).

    Lecture or Reading (15 minutes)

    Present a more detailed explanation of run-length encoding, storing images on computers, and other image compression techniques. There are several ways you can present this information:

    • Have students watch the video of the Mobile CSP version of the lecture
    • Use the slides to give the lecture yourself.
    • Have students read the text-based version of the lesson.

    If you are new to teaching Mobile CSP, we suggest starting by having students watch the video lecture. As you become more familiar with the lesson and its content, you may decide to use the slides to give the lecture on your own, or guide students through reading the text-based lesson.

    PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT

    There are generally two types of image compression techniques. Run-length encoding is a lossless technique, which means that the image is perfectly represented and no data are lost. It is the technique used in TIFF images. It is preferable for medical imaging, archival imaging, clip art, comics, and other applications.

    Lossy compression is not perfect. Some data may be lost. JPEG is an example of lossy compression. It is suitable for photographs and other applications where the loss of some data would be imperceptible.

    The trade-off is between storage required for the image vs. fidelity or quality. If a lot of compression is required -- perhaps because of transmission requirements -- a lossy technique may be preferred.

    Other Possible Activities

    1. Have students take turns encoding messages in ASCII binary code that someone else could decode. Trade messages in class and decode each others.
    2. Web pages use the HyperText Markup Language (HTML) to encode web pages. Point students to the WWW Consortium's color mixer app and invite them to explore the different colors that are used in Web pages. These colors are using a 24-bit scheme. Some problems to solve might be to figure out the Hex code for pure red? pure green? pure blue?
    3. Have students research other image types (e.g. GIF, PNG, BMP, TIFF, etc.) and then share discuss what they discovered in class. Ask them to compare and contrast the data needed to store information about the images. Include what type of compression is used.
    4. Have students research and discuss the type of image format used by their digital camera or a smartphone or tablet, find out what image representation scheme it uses? Question: How come all images are not the same size?
    5. ASCII is one type of character code, but 128 characters is not enough for today’s computers, which can represent Chinese, Hindi, and scripts from many other languages. Today’s computers use a system called Unicode, which has more than 100,000 different characters and covers more than 100 different scripts (languages). Invite students to use this Unicode converter to convert these Chinese characters to their hexadecimal Unicode values: åè. Convert these Greek letters: οι. Convert these Russian letters: Я ю.
